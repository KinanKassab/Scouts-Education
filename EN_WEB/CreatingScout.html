Foundation of Scouting

Scouting was founded in 1907 by Robert Baden-Powell, who was an officer in the British Army in South Africa. During his service there, Baden-Powell recognized the importance of developing youth skills in survival and community service. Baden-Powell organized the first scouting group in South Africa and began developing scouting principles and activities focusing on challenge, adventure, and community service.

The concept of scouting was quickly adopted and expanded widely around the world. In 1908, Baden-Powell founded the World Scouting Movement and presented his famous book "Scouting for Boys," which contains the principles and practices of scouting. Since then, scouting has grown to become a global movement aimed at developing active and responsible youth through practical learning, community service, and adventure in nature.

Scouting relies on a set of core principles such as duty to others, helping others, belief in God, self-discipline, taking responsibility, and openness to the world. Scouting activities range from camping and hiking in nature to community service and learning life skills.

Scouting exists today in most countries around the world and forms a wide network of local, national, regional, and international organizations working to develop youth and promote values of citizenship, cooperation, and social responsibility.
